Portugal

Portugal's cuisine is a blend of Mediterranean and Atlantic influences, with a rich history dating back to the Age of Exploration. The country's cuisine features seafood, cured meats, bread, wine, and pastries, among other specialties. The following is a list of foods you can eat in Portugal:

  • Bacalhau
  • Cozido
  • Pastel de Nata
  • Francesinha
  • Sardinhas

1. Bacalhau

One of the oldest and most cherished dishes in Portuguese cuisine is bacalhau. It is made with salted cod that has been cooked in a variety of ways after being soaked in water to remove extra salt. Bacalhau dishes are popular throughout Portugal, and each region has a distinctive method of making them.

Bacalhau à Brás, a dish made with shredded cod, potatoes, eggs, and onions, and Bacalhau com Natas, a creamy casserole made with cod, potatoes, cream, and onions, are two examples of popular Bacalhau dishes.

Portuguese fishermen first sailed to Newfoundland to catch cod in the 15th century, beginning the long Portuguese history of bacalhau. After that, the fish was salted and prepared for the lengthy trip home. Bacalhau is a dish that is loved by people of all ages and backgrounds and is still a significant component of Portuguese cuisine and culture today.

2. Cozido

Traditional Portuguese cuisine known as cozido is prized for its hearty and flavorful ingredients. This dish falls under the stew category and is typically made with a variety of meats, vegetables, and beans that are cooked over a long period of time. Depending on the region of Portugal and individual preferences, the ingredients can vary, but some of the usual ones are beef, pork, chicken, sausage, potatoes, carrots, cabbage and chickpeas.

Since it is hearty, reassuring, and filling, cozido is a favourite dish in the winter. It is a standard at many restaurants all over Portugal and frequently served as the main course during family gatherings.

Cozido's history can be traced back to the 16th century, and since then, it has remained a staple of Portuguese cuisine. It is a widely adored dish, and Portugal's rich history and diverse flavours have made it a national treasure.

3. Pastel de Nata

Portugal gave birth to the beloved dessert known as pastel de nata, which has gained international acclaim. A creamy, sweet egg custard flavoured with cinnamon and other spices is baked inside a flaky, buttery crust. With a cup of coffee or tea and a dusting of powdered sugar and cinnamon, Pastel de Nata is typically served warm.

The dessert's illustrious past begins in the early 19th century, when monks in Lisbon invented it. The pastry's recipe was kept a closely guarded secret, but it eventually left the monastery's walls and gained popularity among the locals. People of all ages enjoy Pastel de Nata, which can now be found in bakeries and cafes all over Portugal.

The Pastel de Nata has attained widespread acclaim and turned into a representation of Portuguese culture and cuisine. It is a must-try pastry for anyone travelling to Portugal, and many people from all over the world have tried to make their own versions of the recipe. Pastel de Nata has won the hearts and palates of people all over the world despite its straightforward ingredients and modest beginnings, solidifying its position as one of the most adored desserts in the entire world.

4. Francesinha

A national symbol in Portugal, the Francesinha is a traditional dish from Porto. A hearty tomato and beer sauce, melted cheese and layers of bread, gammon, linguica sausage and steak complete the sandwich. The sandwich is a popular dish among both locals and visitors because it is frequently served with a side of french fries and a cold beer.

The first Francesinha was made in the 1960s by a Portuguese immigrant who had just returned from France and wanted to combine the flavours of both nations in one sandwich. The outcome was a filling and tasty sandwich that quickly gained popularity in Porto and elsewhere. The sandwich has many variations today, with every restaurant giving the recipe their own spin.

Francesinha has become a symbol of Portuguese cuisine and culture, and many restaurants in Porto specialize in serving this iconic dish. It is a must-try for anyone visiting the city and is often cited as one of the best sandwiches in the world. Whether enjoyed as a hearty lunch or a late-night snack, Francesinha is sure to satisfy any appetite and leave a lasting impression on anyone who tries it.

5. Sardinhas

Grilled sardines, or sardinhas, are a popular dish in Portugal, especially in the summer. Fresh sardines are used in the recipe; they are salted, then grilled until crispy on the outside and tender on the inside. Usually, sardinhas are served with boiled potatoes, a straightforward salad, and some crusty bread.

As the nation has a long history of seafood and fishing dishes, sardinhas have a long history in Portugal. In the country, grilled sardines rose to popularity in the middle of the 20th century and have remained a standard ever since. Sardines are traditionally grilled and served throughout the city of Lisbon during the well-known Portuguese festival of Santo António, which is associated with the dish in particular.

Many eateries and cafes all over Portugal focus on serving sardinhas, which have come to represent Portuguese cuisine and culture. It is frequently referred to as one of the most delectable and iconic dishes in Portugal and is a must-try for anyone travelling there.
